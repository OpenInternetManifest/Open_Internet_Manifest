**Robbers Cave Experiment (Muzafer Sherif, 1954)**

22 normal, healthy 11-12 year old boys from stable middle-class families were brought to a summer camp in Oklahoma.  
They didn’t know they were part of a psychological study.

**Phase 1 – Group formation**  
The boys were randomly divided into two groups (“Eagles” and “Rattlers”). They had no contact with the other group. Within days strong group identities, flags, slogans and loyalty emerged.

**Phase 2 – Conflict**  
The two groups were set against each other through competition (sports, treasure hunts, etc.).  
Within days real hostility broke out:
- Name-calling, insults and mockery
- Raids on each other’s cabins
- Burning each other’s flags
- Refusing to eat together

The hatred became so intense that the researchers had to physically separate the boys.

**Phase 3 – Reconciliation**  
Sherif first tried joint activities (watching a movie, eating together) — it only made things worse.  
Only when he introduced **superordinate goals** — goals that neither group could achieve alone (fixing a broken water supply, pushing a broken truck to get food) — did the hostility slowly disappear. The boys started helping each other, playing together and forming friendships again.

**The hard lesson**

Group identity and “Us vs Them” thinking can be created extremely quickly — often in just a few days.  
It has little to do with real differences or bad character.  
It mainly has to do with:
- Group loyalty
- Competition for scarce resources
- A narrative that frames the other as the enemy

And it can be undone by common goals that are bigger than the group itself.

**How is this used today?**

- Politics and media constantly create new “Us vs Them” stories (left vs right, vaccinated vs unvaccinated, native vs immigrant, etc.).
- Identity politics strengthens group loyalty and makes real dialogue almost impossible.
- Social media algorithms feed this by showing us mostly content that confirms our own group and demonizes the other.

Not by creating even more polarization, but by focusing on superordinate goals — goals bigger than our own bubble.
