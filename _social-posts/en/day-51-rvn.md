### Stanford Prison Experiment (Philip Zimbardo, 1971)

24 healthy, psychologically stable students were randomly assigned as “prisoners” or “guards” in a mock prison in the basement of Stanford University.  
The experiment was supposed to last two weeks.

It was stopped after **6 days**.

**What happened?**

- The “guards” quickly began humiliating, intimidating, and psychologically abusing the prisoners.
- The “prisoners” rapidly became depressed, passive, and some suffered real emotional breakdowns.
- The roles became so powerful that the guards grew increasingly sadistic and the prisoners started behaving like real inmates.

Important: the participants were not sadists or criminals. They were ordinary students.

**Narratief:**  
“That was an extreme experiment from the 1970s. We would never do that.”

**Realiteit:**  
The experiment shows how quickly normal people adapt to the role they are assigned.  
The situation (the power structure) proved far stronger than personality.

Zimbardo himself later said:  
“We discovered that good people can do bad things simply by being placed in a bad situation.”

**How is this used today?**

- Companies and organizations create roles and structures that elicit certain behaviour (call centres, bureaucracy, social media moderation).
- Governments and institutions put people in roles of “controller” versus “controlled”.
- Social media reinforces this: you are given a role (victim, activist, perpetrator, hero) and you start behaving accordingly.

**The OIM-lesson:**

If you allow yourself to be placed in a role within a corrupt or manipulative system, you will change too.  
That’s why it’s so important not only to complain about “the system”, but to actively build parallel structures in which healthy roles and responsibilities are possible.

Because if you keep playing along in a sick prison, you will eventually become either guard or prisoner — and often both.
